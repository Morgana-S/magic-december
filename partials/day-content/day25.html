Christmas Day

Christmas is a truly special holiday that brings people together in a spirit of joy, love, and generosity, creating cherished memories with family and friends. The warmth of festive traditions, from decorating trees to sharing meals and exchanging gifts, makes it a magical time to celebrate the beauty of giving and connection, inviting everyone to partake in the joy of the season.

Christmas Day is celebrated on December 25th by over two billion people worldwide. Traditionally, it marks the birth of Jesus Christ, but many non-religious individuals and those from different faiths also enjoy the day as a cultural celebration. The term "Christmas" comes from the Old English "Crīstesmæsse," which means Christ's Mass. Today, it is a public holiday in most countries, with only a handful not recognising it as such.

Christmas Traditions

Christmas customs vary widely across the globe and have evolved significantly over time, often blending with other cultural traditions. One of the most cherished customs is gift-giving, which traces its origins back to the Magi who presented gifts to Jesus shortly after his birth. Unfortunately, this tradition has also led to the commercialization of Christmas, with Americans spending an average of $700 on gifts, amounting to a staggering $465 billion overall. Recently, there has been a movement encouraging people to simplify the holiday and refocus on its true meaning.

Popular Traditions
  • Candy Canes: While simple candy sticks existed as early as the 1600s, the iconic hooked candy cane became popular in 1920 thanks to Bob McCormack from Georgia, who added a red and white striped twist.
  • Poinsettias: This vibrant plant became associated with Christmas in the 1920s in the U.S., inspired by a Mexican legend about a girl who offered weeds to celebrate Jesus's birthday, which miraculously bloomed into beautiful red flowers
  • Christmas Trees: The tradition of decorating trees during winter celebrations dates back to ancient times. Martin Luther, a German preacher in the 16th century, is credited with being one of the first to place a tree inside his home for Christmas.
  • Christmas Cards: In Victorian England, Sir Henry Cole invented the holiday card out of necessity in 1843. He commissioned an illustration from his friend J.C. Horsley and sent out the first cards via Penny Post.
  • Caroling: Originally linked to wassailing—a term for a mulled drink—caroling evolved into visiting homes during winter months to sing songs, often receiving warm beverages in return.
Observing Christmas

Many Christmas traditions take place in the lead-up to December 25th. On Christmas morning, families typically gather to see what Santa has brought before sharing gifts around a beautifully decorated tree or enjoying a festive meal together. Other common activities include:

  • Taking photos with Santa
  • Baking cookies and treats
  • Hanging lights and making ornaments
  • Attending holiday concerts
  • Watching festive films
  • Opening Advent calendars

Christmas Day often culminates in a special dinner filled with delicious desserts. The beauty of Christmas lies in its flexibility; families can choose which traditions to uphold or even create new ones. Historical Context

The exact date of Jesus's birth remains uncertain; however, the first recorded celebration of Christmas occurred in 336 AD during Emperor Constantine's reign. Early references to this celebration can be found in writings by John Chrysostom, a 4th-century archbishop. Over time, Christmas adopted various customs from Winter Solstice celebrations, such as decorating with evergreens and feasting. During medieval times, Christmas was observed more solemnly than it is today. The term "Cristes Maesse" was first documented in 1038 in Saxon England. As Christianity spread across Europe, many pagan customs were incorporated into Christmas festivities. In America, Christmas became a federal holiday about a century after the country's founding. By the mid-to-late 1800s, advancements in communication and transportation began uniting diverse regional customs into a more cohesive celebration. Louis Prang introduced the American Christmas card in 1875, gradually replacing personal visits and letters. Influential works like Clement Moore's poem "An Account of Visit from Saint Nicholas" (1823) and Charles Dickens' "A Christmas Carol" (1843) helped shape our modern vision of Christmas, emphasising family togetherness and generosity during this joyful season.
